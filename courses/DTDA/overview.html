Welcome to CCCS 610 Digital thinking for data analysis!

First of all, this is not a programming course in the traditional sense. Yes, we will work with code, specifically Python. No, you do not need to learn all of it from scratch and nobody expects you to be a self-sufficient developer by the end of the term. Our goal is to get everyone comfortable with how computers can be leveraged to represent and understand diverse situations. Our main interest is in learning to read code rather than to write it, and then to make gradually larger modifications to that code in order to achieve specific objectives we set out for ourselves.

Assumed previous knowledge

For students of DACS, this is intended to be the entry point into the certificate. Since we do not force students to take the courses in a particular order, some participants may have taken the other mandatory courses of DACS (CCCS 620 and CCCS 630) prior to this course or may be registered to one or both simultaneously. Not to worry, we strive to accomodate students who progress at different rhythms and orders. Make sure to speak out whenever you feel like we are covering too much ground too quickly so we can jointly discuss what concepts would make good self-study topics and into what depth one should go in each of them.

Assessments

There are no exams, there are no projects, there is no explicit group work. Collaborating on assessments is encouraged, although all sources (including discussions with fellow participants) must be properly cited in all submitted work and the participant must be the original author of any submitted text, data, illustrations, or program code that is not clearly cited to be from an external source.

Each week, there is an assessment that follows up on the topic of the live-class session. You have two options, each time. You can choose either the hands-on path or the conceptual journey (it is perfectly okay to fluctuate from one choice to another each week, if you prefer).

First, you undertake a basic stage that should take you roughly four hours of total independent work (preparation, execution, documentation, submission). If you find yourself spending much more time on it and have difficulties finding out how to be more efficient, please reach out in class or during office hours for orientation. Satisfactory completion of each basic stage has you all set for passing the course with B- to stay in the program; the basic stage is graded on a scale from zero to five points. For more details on the grading rubrics, please consult the course outline.

After the basic stage, if you are particularly interested in the topic or aim for an A, you proceed to the in-depth stage that should take you up to three hours of additional independent study effort and land you up to two more points.
